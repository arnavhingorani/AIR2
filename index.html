Understanding the Five Levels

Level 1: No Artificial Intelligence

What it means:

AI should not be used in any form for this assignment. This level is typically reserved for in-class work where you demonstrate your own knowledge and skills.

Why it matters:

Some assignments require you to show what you personally know and can do. This helps teachers assess your individual understanding and ensures fair evaluation during assessments.

Examples:

In-class essays, exams, quizzes, timed writing assignments, or any work specifically designated as "no AI" by your teacher.

Level 2: Research and Formatting

What it means:

AI can be used as a research assistant, similar to using a search engine like Google. You can also use it to help organize and format your thoughts in a clear, constructive way.

How to use it:

Ask AI to find information on topics, explain concepts you're studying, suggest ways to organize your ideas, or help structure an outline. The content and ideas must still be your own.

Examples:

"Explain the causes of the Civil War," "Help me create an outline for my essay on climate change," or "What are some credible sources about renewable energy?"

Level 3: Revisions

What it means:

AI helps improve work you've already created. Think of it as a tutor or editor who provides suggestions for grammar, word choice, clarity, and style after you've written your first draft.

How to use it:

Write your essay, paragraph, or response first. Then, use AI to check for grammatical errors, suggest better vocabulary, improve sentence structure, or clarify confusing passages. You review and approve all changes.

Examples:

"Review this paragraph for grammar errors," "Suggest a better word than 'good' in this sentence," or "Is this thesis statement clear?"

Level 4: Idea Creation

Important: This level ALWAYS requires explicit teacher permission before use. You must also document how you used AI in your work.

What it means:

AI helps you brainstorm and develop ideas, but you must transform these suggestions into your own original work. This is collaborative thinking, not copying.

How to use it:

Use AI to generate initial ideas, explore different angles on a topic, or overcome creative blocks. Then, develop these ideas into something uniquely yours.

Examples:

"Give me five creative angles for a story about time travel," "What are some unique perspectives on this historical event?" or "Help me brainstorm project ideas about environmental science."

Level 5: Content Generation

Important: This level ALWAYS requires explicit teacher permission. You must clearly acknowledge and cite which parts were AI-assisted, just like citing any other source.

What it means:

AI becomes a co-creator in your work, generating significant portions of content that you then edit, customize, and integrate into your project.

How to use it:

AI generates content based on your specifications, which you then refine, verify for accuracy, personalize, and properly attribute. You remain responsible for all final content.

Examples:

Using AI to draft sections of code for a programming project, generate initial paragraphs for an essay that you heavily revise, or create data visualizations that you customize.

Important Guidelines to Remember

  • Default Permissions: By default, Levels 1, 2, and 3 are permitted in every class unless your teacher specifies otherwise.
  • In-Class vs. Homework: Level 1 (No AI) is typically reserved for in-class assignments, since AI use cannot realistically be monitored outside of school.
  • Permission Required: Levels 4 and 5 ALWAYS require teacher permission before use. Using these levels without permission is a violation of academic integrity.
  • Proper Citation: When using Levels 4 or 5, you must properly cite AI use in your work. Include the AI tool used, the date used, and a brief description of how it was used.
  • Academic Integrity: These guidelines exist to help you learn effectively while using AI responsibly. Misusing AI or failing to follow these guidelines is considered academic dishonesty.
  • When in Doubt: If you're ever unsure which level applies to your assignment or whether AI use is appropriate, ASK YOUR TEACHER.

Subject-Specific Guidelines

Clear guidance on how to use AI appropriately in each subject area.

English

✓ Acceptable Use

Grammar and spelling checks after writing your own draft
Practicing literary analysis with AI as a discussion partner (clearly noted)
Using AI to properly format and create citations

✗ Not Acceptable Use

Having AI write essays, paragraphs, or interpretations
Using AI to summarize or analyze literature instead of reading it
Asking AI to generate essay topics instead of brainstorming your own

Math

✓ Acceptable Use

Checking final answers or graphing completed problems
Getting step-by-step explanations after solving manually
Using AI to explore alternate solution methods or compare approaches

✗ Not Acceptable Use

Using AI to complete full problem sets or generate fake work steps
Copying AI-generated answers without understanding the reasoning

Science

✓ Acceptable Use

Using AI to brainstorm research topics
Asking AI to explain complex scientific concepts
Using AI to find credible sources and format citations appropriately

✗ Not Acceptable Use

Using AI to create fake data, results, or lab observations
Having AI write lab reports, discussions, or hypotheses

Social Studies

✓ Acceptable Use

Summarizing primary sources or organizing research notes
Using AI to grade or critique practice AP History SAQs or essays
Using AI to fact-check, generate timelines, or build study guides

✗ Not Acceptable Use

Having AI write essays, arguments, or historical analyses
Using AI to fabricate or misrepresent sources or citations

Foreign Language

✓ Acceptable Use

Practicing pronunciation or conversation with AI tools
Checking grammar and vocabulary after writing your own sentences
Using AI to explore cultural context, idioms, or regional differences

✗ Not Acceptable Use

Using AI translators or chatbots to complete graded work
Copying or rephrasing AI-generated sentences as your own

These guidelines are designed to help you use AI as a learning tool while maintaining academic integrity.

Why the AIR Guidelines Exist

The integration of AI into education and the workforce is accelerating rapidly. Arizona's students need clear guidelines to navigate this transformation ethically and effectively, preparing them for a future where AI literacy is essential.

Where the Job Market is Going

According to UPCEA and the World Economic Forum:

323% increase in hiring for AI roles over eight years
66% of business leaders won't hire candidates without AI skills
75% prefer less experienced candidates with AI skills
Employers increasingly use AI to reduce costs, making AI literacy essential

AI Literacy Matters

Stanford's High School Cheating Survey reveals:

AI literacy education correlates with lower AI-specific misuse
Students with proper training integrate AI more ethically
Education through workshops on ethical use and limitations is crucial

Data Science Literacy

Data Science Literacy is the baseline for understanding AI.

Arizona is a Tier 0 State as of 2024, in the research and development phase
Lagging behind 92% of states for Data Science Literacy
No announced statewide plans for course catalogs or adoptions
No tracked K-12 student enrollment in dedicated data science courses
Arizona lacks formal learning standards for DS, weakening AI policies

Our Response

These guidelines represent a student-driven initiative to bridge the gap, providing a framework for ethical AI use while preparing Arizona's students to compete in an AI-driven world. By establishing clear standards now, we're taking a proactive step toward building AI literacy from the ground up.
